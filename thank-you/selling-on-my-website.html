Selling on Your Website


Now that you have decided what to sell online, the next step is to focus on figuring out where to sell. While there are a lot of options, only a few might be best suited for you depending on your needs. Each platform or option has its own advantages and disadvantages, so it pays to research into a few options before deciding which one to use to start selling online.

The platforms can be broadly classified into three categories: self-designed store, marketplace, sell via dealer/distributor. Let’s briefly look into what each does.

Selling on Your Website

These platforms empower you to run your own exclusive online store. These platforms also connect the seller with the most optimal and cost-efficient logistics partner to fulfill customer orders anywhere in the world.

Marketplace

Marketplaces are the platforms which empower sellers to list their inventory and set up their online store on a website or app managed by a marketplace. As the name suggests, an online marketplace is a virtual market-like setup where different sellers and businesses can set up their virtual shops on a property (domain or website) owned and managed by another company. Buyers or consumers come to the marketplace because it offers a large selection of products that they may not find on individual online stores. Best examples of marketplaces are Amazon and eBay.

The pros of the marketplace are:

  1. Less Work - You don’t have to worry about creating and building your own online store. You can leverage what marketplace has already built to list your products. You don’t have to worry about having the right technology infrastructure to support the traffic and maintain a very high (99.99%) uptime. And, you don’t have to worry about bringing traffic to your product listing page. Marketplace does all the heavy lifting to bring traffic to its website. All you have to focus on is optimizing your product listing page so that the traffic, once on the marketplace, can easily find your products.

  2. Low Commission to Collect Payment - 0-to-low commission charged to collect payment from customers across the globe.

  3. Outsource Storage and Fulfillment - Depending on the marketplace, you don’t have to worry about fulfilling the customer order and/or storing your inventory. For example, the Fulfillment by Amazon (FBA) allows sellers to store their inventory in an Amazon warehouse, and once the order is placed then the product is shipped to the customer by Amazon. On the other hand, eBay allows sellers to list their inventory/product on eBay but the storage of inventory and fulfillment of customer order has to be managed by the seller.

  4. Scaling Globally - Depending on the marketplace, it’s easy for you to geographically expand and sell your products anywhere in the world. For example, Amazon allows sellers to sell globally under the Global Selling program.

  5. Low-ish Shipping Rates - Marketplaces benefit from economies of scale, and hence are able to negotiate extremely economical shipping rates, which some, if not all, marketplaces pass on to the sellers. As a result, the sellers can offer extremely low shipping rates to buyers, which the seller might not be able to do when selling on their own online store.

The cons of the marketplace are:

  1. Tough to Differentiate Identity and Brand - It’s tough to have your unique identity and brand when selling on marketplaces. Online marketplaces are extremely crowded as you have to compete with thousand other sellers or businesses offering similar products as yours. It’s tough to be within the top 3 search results of any product, and one has to optimize the product listings to be found as quickly as possible by the potential buyers and customers. As a seller on a marketplace, you’re one among thousands, if not millions, and you’re on equal footing with every other seller as every seller gets the same real estate to showcase their products. Customers of the marketplace rarely come there for any particular company. Instead, customers are interested in getting the best product regardless of the brand as quickly as possible.

  2. Extremely High Commission - Depending on the marketplace, a high commission is charged per order or per transaction whenever the sale happens. The commission further increases when the marketplace offers storage and fulfillment options. The commissions can run anywhere between 5% to 30% per sale. This is the commission charged when selling locally. When international transactions are included then the commission can range anywhere between 10% to 40% per sale. For a lot of sellers, high commissions are a bitter pill to swallow as it eats into their profitability.

  3. No Customer Relationship - Very difficult to manage direct customer relationships, engage with the customers and run loyalty programs for them. Marketplace owns the customer relationship, and any communication that seller has with the buyer. Due to privacy concerns and various policies of the marketplaces, sellers rarely know who the buyer is, what he or she likes and dislikes. Every transaction between the seller and buyer is positioned as a one-time transaction.

  4. No Freedom to Set Your Own Policies - Can’t set a refund policy as you desire. When you sell on marketplace, you’re bound by the rules set by the marketplace, which also includes when refund can be issued and how much refund can be issued to the customers. Marketplaces, more likely, benefit buyers. If a marketplace were to favor sellers when it comes to refunds then the buyers would most likely stop buying from that marketplace, and buyers would start exploring other buyer-friendly marketplaces. The more buyers the marketplace has, the more money the marketplace makes.

  5. Can’t Get Your Money Faster - When you sell on the marketplace, you may not be able to instantly get your money and realize your working capital. Many marketplaces have a policy of settling, i.e. remitting money into seller’s account, once a week to once every 15 days. Unlike selling on your own online store where you can see money remitted to your account once a day to once every two days.

  6. No Freedom to Deliver Unique Experiences - You can’t create the kind of online experience you want to create for your customers. When you sell on marketplaces, you’re bound by the online experience and shopping experience delivered by the marketplace. It's the same for every seller and hence you cannot differentiate yourself.

  7. Scaling Globally - Depending on the marketplaces, when selling on a marketplace, you may not be able to scale globally as fast as you want. Not every marketplace offers global selling, and not every marketplace is in many countries. Amazon and eBay are exceptions to this. If you want to target customers across the globe then you’re better off being on Amazon or eBay, or having your own online store.


Sell via Distributor/Dealer

Rarely practiced in recent times, this is similar to selling on marketplaces in some ways. When selling via distributor/dealer, the seller sells the manufactured product to the distributor/dealer. Alternately, the seller sends the manufactured product to the distributor/dealer who then lists the inventory on their website. When a seller sends the manufactured product to the distributor/dealer, both can negotiate a revenue-share agreement and the list price of the product. The distributor/dealer may or may not list the seller name on the distributor/dealer website. Distributor/dealer does the heavy lifting of bringing traffic to the site, and making the sale happen. Once an order is placed, the distributor/dealer fulfills the customer order. For all purposes, the seller doesn’t interact with the buyer, it’s the distributor/dealer who manages the customer relationship. This is very similar to auto dealerships.

Key factors to consider when looking for a platform to set up your online business:

  1. If it’s a platform that offers a self-designed store then how many customers - just like you - the platform has? The more customers, the stronger the confidence in the platform.

  2. If it’s a marketplace, how many customers or buyers does it have? The more customers or buyers the marketplace has, the more potential customers for your business.

  3. If it’s a marketplace, how many sellers - just like you - does it have? The more sellers, particularly in your product segment, the marketplace has, the more confidence in the marketplace but also more competition you will have. potential customers for your business. It’s a good practice to assess the number of sellers selling products just like yours with the number of potential buyers on the marketplace.

  4. What are different costs the seller will incur to use the platform? What are the different fees the seller has to pay? What are variable costs and fixed costs of using the platform?

  5. If it’s a marketplace, is the platform best suited for the type and category of products you’re selling? Imagine selling toys on Etsy, or fresh produce on eBay?

  6. How reliable is the platform? How good is its customer service? How fast can they resolve customer issues? Will this platform exist 10 years down the line? These are some questions you must find answers to.

  7. If it’s a marketplace, how seller-friendly the platform is? How flexible is it with a seller who has just started the business? How flexible is the platform itself? Some marketplaces offer no flexibility and some have strict guidelines that simple deviation from those guidelines lead to account suspension. This is not a problem when you’re selling on your own self-designed store.

There are a few factors you must keep in mind to ensure long-term success.

Highly Optimized Product Listing

Customers or buyers spend a lot of time reading the product description and product details. It’s imperative that you clearly articulate the benefits that customers will get by buying your product, and the benefits the customers will miss by not buying your product. Inject some fun and creativity into your listings that connect emotionally with the buyer. It’s also important to have high quality product images and videos that show products from all angles, and that help buyers visualize what they’re going to get by owning such a product. It’s no surprise that millions of buyers decide to buy something simply based on photos and videos. Great photos and videos are the easiest way to set yourself apart.

Social Proof

Social proof, aka validation or reviews from other customers and buyers about your product, generates trust in buyers and gives them confidence that what you’re selling is an amazing quality product. Whenever possible, request your customers to leave reviews as this helps in convincing your future customers to buy your products.

Kick-ass Marketing Strategy

You should have a plan for how to promote your business outside of your chosen platform so that you can attract more customers to your listings and make more sales. This should include organic marketing such as search engine optimization and content marketing, and paid marketing such as ads on various platforms such as Google, Facebook, Youtube, Pinterest etc.

Fastest Fulfillment

Customers expect to receive their products as quickly as possible, many times within the same day, and often the next day. In fact, it has been seen that customers end up purchasing more whenever same day or 1-day delivery is offered. The added convenience of getting products quickly, within a few hours of placing the order, influences the customers to shop more often from that online store compared to going to other stores. Regardless of what selling model you use, it’s imperative that you choose a platform that empowers you to deliver your products as quickly as possible to your customers. The only exception is when you lower prices so low that lower prices compensate for longer delivery times.

Delightful Customer Experience

This goes without saying that the customer is the king and customers are your business’ lifeblood. Treat your customer as if it’s your only customer and that without them your business will perish.
