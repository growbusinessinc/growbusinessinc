Set up Business

Once you know what you are selling, where you are selling and how you are selling, the next step is formalize this by setting up a business entity. If you’re not ready yet to form a legal business entity yet then no problem. You can keep testing and validating your business idea until you are sure to formally set it up. While validating your business idea, ensure that you as revenue starts trickling in, you would have to start paying taxes.

When you’re ready to formally set up a business, there are a couple of regulatory and compliance requirements you need to take care off. Starting a business includes understanding and dealing with many issues such as legal, finance, intellectual property protection, liability protection, compliance with regulations, and more. Every country has different requirements for setting up a business, it’s a best practice to speak to an accountant (CPA, CA etc.) or a lawyer to get professional advice.

Choose the type of business you want to set up

This is very dependent on the laws of the country. For example, in the United States, a business entity can be set up as Sole Proprietorship, Partnership, S corporation or S corp, C corporation or C corp, or as a Limited Liability Company (LLC). These days any type of business entity can be set up easily and quickly. Depending on the type of business entity chosen, you may or may not be protected from personal liability.

Let’s learn a little bit about each of these business entities.

Sole Proprietorship

Sole proprietorships are intended for simple, one-owner businesses. For example, freelancers, consultants, food trucks, one-person eCommerce stores or online businesses, etc. Sole Proprietorships do not have shares or ownership units which means the only exit option is to sell the assets of the company. Sole proprietorships are by far the simplest type of entity to set up. There really is no structure. Sole proprietors with no employees do not even need to register with the Internal Revenue Service in the United States. They can simply use their Social Security Numbers as the business tax ID. There is generally no need to “incorporate” to deduct business expenses. By default, a legitimate business that starts without formally incorporating is a Sole Proprietorship, unless there is more than one owner, in which case it’s a Partnership, and is eligible to deduct its business expenses. When it comes to taxation, Sole Proprietor owes personal income tax on the entire profits of the business regardless of whether those profits have been withdrawn or not.

Partnership

Whenever a Sole Proprietorship has more than one owner then it’s a Partnership. Just like Sole Proprietorship, business entities that are Partnership are very easy to set up. Partnership business entity is used by early-stage businesses that have not yet achieved significant profitability. The arrangement can be especially attractive for small companies without employees, where the owners do most of the work. While Partnership is simple, they require a few key things for smooth running of Partnership as a business entity. It is important to have a partnership operating agreement that controls the operations and ownership of the company. In Partnerships, the business income does not need to be allocated proportionately to ownership. This flexibility can be helpful when there is a very silent partner who contributed most of the capital but is not expecting a similar share of the profits. Any such arrangement must be clearly laid out in a partnership agreement. When it comes to taxation, Partnership owes tax on the entire taxable income of the Partnership, and it’s generally subject to FICA taxes. In addition, the co-owners in the Partnership are subjected to personal income taxes.

S Corporation

As companies grow and become profitable they tend to choose to become a S Corporation. S corporations are a very popular entity choice for small and mid-sized privately held companies. S corporations do not pay any income taxes. Instead, the corporation's income and losses are divided among and passed through to its shareholders. The shareholders must then report the income or loss on their own individual income tax returns. Unique characteristics of S Corporation are:

  1. You must generally be a US resident or citizen to own an interest in an S corporation. That is a deal breaker for companies seeking corporate or foreign investors (e.g. startups seeking venture capital funding). Certain trusts and estates are allowed as stockholders, but partnerships and corporations may not own a stake in an S corporation.

  2. Profits and distributions must always be allocated according to ownership.

  3. In some cases, an owner of an S corporation that has losses may not be able to deduct that loss on their personal tax return. The loss would be carried forward to a future year, but most startups would appreciate the extra cash from a tax refund today, not in some future year. A partnership or proprietorship structure is generally more favorable to claiming such losses.

  4. Only one class of stock is allowed. There can be voting and non-voting shares but classes of preferred and common stock are not allowed.

  5. There can be a maximum of 100 stockholders.


C Corporation

If you are thinking of C Corporation then you are a publicly traded entity or about to become one. You have grown really big. All large American publicly traded corporations are C corporations. It is the only entity form that works for them. Another group that utilizes the C corporation structure are high-growth startups seeking series funding because their target investors may be entities or foreign individuals, neither of which are allowed to invest in an S corporation. C corporations are unique in that the corporation pays its own income tax. When it comes to taxation, while there is double taxation, the entity itself pays low taxes on the company's profits. Shareholders of a C corporation generally must pay tax on the dividends they withdraw from the corporation. Essentially the C corporation pays tax on its income first, and the remaining money is distributed to the owners, who pay tax on it again.

Limited Liability Company (LLC)

An LLC is not a corporation under state law; it is a legal form of a company that provides limited liability to its owners in many jurisdictions. LLCs are well known for the flexibility that they provide to business owners; depending on the situation, an LLC may elect to use corporate tax rules instead of being treated as a partnership. A limited liability company (LLC) is a hybrid legal entity having certain characteristics of both a corporation and a partnership or sole proprietorship (if there is only one owner). As a business entity, an LLC is often more flexible than a corporation and may be well-suited for companies with a single owner. The owner(s) of an LLC must choose one of the other four structures as their identity for tax purposes. Compared to Partnerships and Sole Proprietorships, the LLC structure helps shield the owner’s personal assets from a business lawsuit, and informs the world that the owner is not personally liable for claims. Compared to S Corp and C Corp, an LLC structure is generally simpler to administrate. For example, true corporations are often required to hold annual meetings and keep records of meeting minutes. An LLC taxed as a corporation is generally not subject to these regulations. Starting out as an LLC gives a company flexibility for a later entity change. For example, a common path is to form an LLC first, have it taxed as a partnership, then elect S corporation status after the company becomes profitable.


Moving to India, a business entity can be set up as One Person Company, Limited Liability Partnership (LLP), Private Limited Company, and Public Limited Company. These days any type of business entity can be set up easily and quickly. Depending on the type of business entity chosen, you may or may not be protected from personal liability.

Let’s learn a little bit about each of these business entities.

One Person Company

This is best suited when there is only one owner of the company. For example, freelancers, consultants, food trucks, one-person eCommerce stores or online businesses, etc. Similar to Sole Proprietorship in the US, One Person Company do not have shares or ownership units which means the only exit option is to sell the assets of the company, and are by far the simplest type of entity to set up. When it comes to taxation, there is a tax holiday for the first 3 years under Startup India, they receive higher benefits on depreciation, and no tax paid on dividend distribution, and when applicable, business taxes need to be filed.

Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

This is best suited for firms in Service-oriented businesses or businesses that have low investment needs. A separate legal entity, in an LLP the liabilities of partners are only limited only to their agreed contribution. When it comes to taxation, they get the benefit on depreciation and business taxes need to be filed.

Private Limited Company

These are the businesses with significant revenue. Private Limited Company is regarded as a separate legal entity from its founders in the eyes of the law. It has shareholders and directors (company officers). Each individual is regarded as an employee of the company. When it comes to taxation, there is a tax holiday for the first 3 years under Startup India, they receive higher benefits on depreciation, and business taxes need to be filed. In addition, there are mandatory audit requirements.

Public Limited Company

A public limited company in India is similar to C Corp in the United States. It’s publicly traded, is strictly regulated with mandatory audits, and is required to publish its true financial health to its shareholders periodically.

Choose the name of the business

Once you have decided what type of business entity you want, the next step is to choose an appropriate name for your business. This name is your brand. The name should reflect what you and your company stand for, and it should be something that you are comfortable broadcasting all over the world. It’s also important that the name of your business should be such that your potential customers should not find it offensive or embarrassing. The business name should also be easy to spell and easy to remember. Selecting the right name for your startup can have a significant impact on your business success. The wrong name could result in insurmountable legal and business hurdles.

Get a Tax ID

Depending on the type of business entity, you will have to register and get a tax ID for your business. A Tax ID is a number you obtain from your state and/or local government that allows you to charge sales tax whenever a buyer in your state, county and/or city purchases an item from you, even if it’s an online purchase. This is also known as an Employer Identification Number (EIN) in the United States, or Company Registration Number (CIN) in India. It’s similar to a Social Security Number (SSN) of the United States or Personal Account Number (PAN), but for businesses. Banks will ask for your Tax ID number when you open a company bank account.

Get Appropriate Licenses

This is dependent on what you sell and how you decide to sell. Not every product sold requires licenses or go through tons of bureaucracy. Following are some of the licenses or permits you may have to get:

  1. Permits meant for highly regulated businesses such as alcohol, farming, medicines etc.

  2. Permit to run home-based businesses from your home

  3. Zoning permit

  4. Seller’s permit

  5. Health Department and Food Department permits (food, grocery etc.)

Set up a Accounting/Bookkeeping System

Once the revenue starts trickling in, an accounting/bookkeeping system is required to keep track of income, outflow, expenses, CAPEX, EBITDA, profit and loss statements, assets, debt etc. This helps you learn your business’ cash flow and identify ways to further optimize revenue and profit.

Protect Your Intellectual Property

Often ignored by entrepreneurs, it’s important to protect your intellectual property so that you can maintain a high barrier to entry for other companies/entrepreneurs. If you don’t then it’s easy for any new entrant to copy you and drive you out. Couple of ways to protect the intellectual property:

Patents - Patents are the best protection you can get for a new product. A patent gives its inventor the right to prevent others from making, using, or selling the patented subject matter described in the patent’s claims. The key factors determining whether one can get a patent are:
a. Only the concrete embodiment of an idea or product is patentable.
b. The invention must be new.
c. The invention must not have been patented or described in a printed publication before.
d. the invention must be useful

Trade Secrets - A trade secret right allows the owner of the right to take action against anyone who breaches an agreement or confidential relationship, or who steals or uses other improper means to obtain secret information. Trade secrets can be anything - an algorithm, a method to manufacture something, customer lists, to even the chemical formula of Coca-Cola and Pepsi.

Trade Secrets - A trademark right protects the symbolic value of a name, symbol, or device that the trademark owner uses to distinguish its goods from those of others. Some well-known trademarks include Coca-Cola, Visa etc. You obtain rights to a trademark by actually using the mark in commerce.

Copyrights - A copyright gives the owner the exclusive right to make copies of the work and to prepare derivative works of the original work Applicable to original works of authorship, such as art, advertising copy, books, articles, music, movies, software, etc.

Confidentiality Agreements - Also called Non Disclosure Agreements, these agreements allow the owner to discuss and share a product or business idea with a third party, while obligating the third party to keep the information confidential and not use it whatsoever, unless allowed by the owner of the information.

Terms of Service - As an online business, it is important to have a terms of service agreement that limits what users can or cannot do on your website and with the information available from your site.
